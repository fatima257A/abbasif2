What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software ("malware") that locks or encrypts a user's data and demands payment, usually in cryptocurrency, to restore access. Even paying doesn't guarantee recovery (Abrams, 2022).

How Ransomware Works

There are two major types: "locker" ransomware, which locks your device, and "crypto" ransomware, which encrypts your files (Scaife et al., 2016). Some types even threaten to leak your private data.

How Ransomware Spreads

Common methods include phishing emails, unsafe websites, fake downloads, and infected USB drives (Hadlington, 2017). Unpatched software vulnerabilities also provide an entry point for attackers.

Early Impacts

Attackers target businesses, hospitals, schools, and governments. In 2020, a ransomware attack on a German hospital led to a patient's death after emergency services were disrupted (Heinrich-Böll-Stiftung, 2021).

Protecting Against Ransomware

How to Protect Yourself Against Ransomware

Prevention Tips

  • Backup your files regularly.
  • Keep your operating system and software updated (NCSC, 2021).
  • Use antivirus software and enable firewalls.
  • Be cautious with emails and downloads.

What To Do If Attacked

Disconnect your device immediately. Contact IT professionals or cybersecurity authorities like the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center. Avoid paying the ransom as it may not guarantee recovery (Europol, 2023).

Importance of Cybersecurity Awareness

Training and awareness campaigns in schools, businesses, and families can significantly reduce risks. Cybersecurity is everyone's responsibility.

Ransomware Trends and Responses

Advanced Ransomware Trends and Responses

Evolution of Ransomware

The first ransomware attack, the "AIDS Trojan," occurred in 1989. Modern attacks use Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS), making it easier for cybercriminals to operate (Scaife et al., 2016; Europol, 2023).

Famous Cases

WannaCry (2017): Infected over 200,000 computers globally.
Colonial Pipeline (2021): Disrupted US fuel supply, costing millions (Abrams, 2022).

Government and Business Responses

Governments are creating stricter laws, and businesses are investing in cyber insurance and response plans. The "No More Ransom" project offers free decryption tools (Europol, 2023).

Conclusion

With trends like "double extortion" and AI-driven attacks on the rise, staying informed, cautious, and prepared is crucial. Cybersecurity is a collective effort, and basic steps can significantly reduce risks.
